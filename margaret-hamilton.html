Margaret Hamilton

Margaret Hamilton is renowned for her work on the Apollo missions at NASA. She is also known as the founder of Higher Order Software and Hamilton Technologies. In 2016 President Barack Obama awarded Margaret Hamilton, aged 79, for her involvement in the development of on-board flight software for NASAs Apollo Moon missions. The software Hamilton helped developed for the Apollo Missions was the most advanced and elaborate of its day.

Margaret Hamilton is an unsung hero whose work made the moon landing of Apollo 11 possible, and she helped pioneer software engineering. Teaching your children about the work of Margaret Hamilton will promote diversity in STEM and inspire girls to enter the world of computer programming!

Margaret Hamilton's early years

Margaret Hamilton was born on the 17th of August 1936 in Paoli, Indiana, USA. Hamilton and her parents, Kenneth Heafield and Ruth Esther Heafield, moved to Michigan after a few years. Hamilton attended Hancock High School and graduated in 1954. She went on to study mathematics at the University of Michigan before transferring to Earlham college, where her mother was also a student. She graduated with a major in mathematics and a minor in philosophy.

While studying at Earlham College, she met James Hamilton and the two married. In the early years of their marriage, Margaret Hamilton taught mathematics in her local High School. She was a teacher for a short time before moving to Boston.

Hamilton was originally going to study abstract mathematics at Brandeis, but instead accepted a job from MIT in the meteorology department. Margaret Hamiltons job involved developing and programming software that would predict the weather.

Early Career

Margaret Hamiltons career quickly began to progress at MIT. In the early 1960s, she began working at MITs Lincoln Laboratory on the SAGE project, which was the first US air defence system. Hamiltons work involved writing software that could identify enemy aircraft. She progressed to MITs Instrumentation Laboratory to work on aeronautical technology for NASA.

Margaret Hamilton led the team that was responsible for inventing and creating the software for the control and guidance systems in the in-flight command and lunar modules of the Apollo missions. Her specific job role was working on a software that would detect and identify system errors, and resolve these errors. The software could also recover information in the case of a computer crash. Hamiltons software was a significant element in the success of Apollo 11. During her time at NASA working on the Apollo missions, Hamilton coined the term software engineering, as she believed her team was equally contributing to the Apollo spacecraft as much as the engineering department was. At the time, software engineering courses, or even computer science courses, were not taught at any institution, meaning any obstacles or uncertainties the team faced had to be resolved by themselves. With no guide books or previous work to build on, their work was the first of its kind. Hamilton was only 32 at the time, but there is no doubt her expertise and hard work went above and beyond.

This was evident when the lunar module was about to land on the moon. Before landing on the moon the spacecrafts radar had accidentally been switched on, this meant the computer was soon receiving too much information and the programme began to overload. An alert was sent back to the station at NASA, and a decision had to be made whether to go ahead with Apollo 11s landing or not. Fortunately, Margaret Hamilton had written a code into the computers programme that enabled it to identify the issue itself and ignore and eliminate the radar information. Back at the NASA station, they identified that the software was working effectively despite the hardware error, meaning landing was still possible. Without Hamiltons code, it is possible Apollo 11 would not have landed.

While working on the software for Apollo, Margaret Hamilton and her team also developed software for Skylab, the first space station to be launched by the US.

To honour her work at NASA, Margaret Hamilton was awarded the Exceptional Space Act Award in 2003.

Post-NASA

After her successful work on the Apollo missions, Hamilton shifted her focus to programming Skylab. She mainly focused on the operating systems and error detection and recovery.

In the mid-1970s, Margaret Hamilton left NASA and co-founded the Higher Order Software company with Saydean Zeldin. They worked together to develop ideas about error prevention technologies. After 10 years, Hamilton left the company and founded Hamilton Technologies, Inc. in 1986. Her company worked with the Universal Systems Language (USL). Hamilton Technologies provided services and products that would modernise and revolutionise the planning and development of system engineering and software.

Legacy

Margaret Hamiltons work has been awarded by countless universities, academic institutions and computer science organisations. Though her contributions to NASA and the success of the Apollo 11 landing, her early work at MIT is also accredited. While working and developing code for the worlds first portable computer, she also assisted in the creation of core principles for computer programming. This means, without Margaret Hamilton, you wouldnt be reading this wiki on your laptop or tablet!

Margaret Hamilton, alongside the other unsung female heroes of her time, opened doors and created opportunities for young women in STEM.

Teach your children all about Margaret Hamilton

Want to teach your young learners about Margaret Hamilton? Then have a look through our incredible collection of resources, activities and lesson materials. All our resources have been designed and created by our team of experienced teachers, based on the aims of the 2014 national curriculum. They will enhance and enrich your science or computing lessons, by supporting your childrens learning and understanding.

Our Science: Scientists and Inventors: Mission to the Moon Year 5 Lesson is a great resource to use if youre introducing your children to Margaret Hamilton and her work for the first time. It focuses on her time at NASA and the work she did to help land Apollo 11. Inside this lesson pack there is a detailed lesson plan, an interactive PowerPoint presentation and resources to help you mark and track your childrens work. Plus, there is a Space Travel Timeline activity for your children to fill in individually or in pairs. Your pupils will need to match up the space travel events with the time to complete the timeline.

You can use this Science Knowledge Organiser: Scientists and Inventors Year 5 alongside the lesson pack. This knowledge organiser will give your pupils an overview of key scientists and inventors, and it includes a summary of Margaret Hamiltons work for NASA.

Your pupils can get creative with this marvellous Margaret Hamilton Colouring Sheet! Not only is colouring page a fun activity your children can do at the end of the lesson or during their free time, it will help pupils develop and improve their fine motor skills. Once your children have finished colouring in, they can take their Margaret Hamilton Colouring Sheet home to their loved ones. They can share what theyve learnt about Margaret Hamilton, too! This is a great way for them to consolidate their learning.
